  <h1>Research</h1>

  <p>
    My research spans aquatic ecology, biodiversity conservation, and socio-ecological systems, with a particular focus on how human activities reshape ecological communities and their management. I am especially interested in the interactions among biological invasions, land-use change, environmental degradation, and local livelihoods in Asia.

<section>
  <h2>Market integration of invasive sailfin catfish</h2>

  <p class="imgcap">
    <img src="{{ '/assets/img/research/market_sold_suckermouth.jpg' | relative_url }}" class="research-photo" alt="Sailfin catfish sold in a local market in Can Tho City, Mekong Delta, Vietnam">
    <br>
    Sailfin catfish (Pterygoplichthys spp.) sold in a local market in Can Tho City, Mekong Delta, Vietnam.
  </p>

  <p>
    Invasive fishes are increasingly incorporated into local food systems, yet the landscape contexts and market mechanisms underlying this process remain poorly understood. We investigated the market occurrence and economic position of sailfin catfish (Pterygoplichthys spp.) in Can Tho City, Mekong Delta, Vietnam. We surveyed 53 local retail markets in 2025, quantified sailfin catfish presence, and linked each market to surrounding land-use/land-cover (LULC) composition within a 1000-m radius.
  </p>

  <p>
    LULC composition was analyzed using Bray-Curtis hierarchical clustering and centered log-ratio (CLR) transformation followed by principal component analysis (PCA). We also analyzed vendor buy/sell prices from six representative markets. Markets were classified into three landscape clusters: a rice-dominated cluster, an urban-dominated cluster, and a woody-crop-dominated cluster.
  </p>

  <p>
    Sailfin catfish occurrence differed significantly among clusters (chi-square test, p = 0.0187; Cramer's V = 0.387), with the highest occurrence in the rice-dominated cluster (78.3%), followed by the woody-crop cluster (43.8%) and the urban cluster (35.7%). Pairwise comparison showed significantly higher occurrence in the rice-dominated than urban cluster.
  </p>

  <p>
    PCA of CLR-transformed LULC identified two major gradients (PC1 = 41.5%, PC2 = 24.4%). In logistic regression, PC1 significantly predicted sailfin catfish occurrence both in the main model controlling for market size (p = 0.0068) and in the LULC-only sensitivity analysis (p = 0.0449), whereas PC2 was not significant.
  </p>

  <p>
    Price data showed that sailfin catfish belonged to the lower-priced group among 39 traded taxa, but matched buy-sell data suggested relatively high vendor markups compared with other fishes.
  </p>

  <p>
    These results indicate that invasive sailfin catfish are most strongly embedded in markets associated with rice-field landscapes and can function as a low-priced but profitable fishery commodity. The study highlights how invasive species can become integrated into food systems through interactions between landscape structure and market incentives in the city.
  </p>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Coral reef bleaching and environmental stressors</h2>

  <p>
    I investigate coral reef bleaching dynamics using long-term field monitoring data and environmental covariates. My interest lies in understanding how local stressors interact with thermal anomalies to shape bleaching severity, recovery, and community-level responses across reef sites.
  </p>

  <p>
    This work combines ecological monitoring, statistical modeling, and site-based comparisons to examine how environmental heterogeneity influences bleaching outcomes. I am particularly interested in identifying locally relevant factors that may help explain why coral assemblages differ in vulnerability even under broadly shared climatic stress.
  </p>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Environmental DNA and fish community ecology</h2>

  <p>
    Another major focus of my research is the application of environmental DNA metabarcoding to freshwater fish communities. Using repeated surveys along riverine gradients, I study spatial and temporal variation in fish assemblages, including patterns of beta diversity, replacement, and nestedness.
  </p>

  <p>
    This line of research aims to understand how fish communities are structured along environmental gradients and how molecular approaches can contribute to biodiversity monitoring and conservation planning in inland waters.
  </p>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Socio-ecological systems and human–nature interactions</h2>

  <p>
    Beyond ecological monitoring, I am interested in how ecosystems are linked to people through livelihood practices, governance, public awareness, and local environmental knowledge. My broader research perspective is shaped by area studies and socio-ecological thinking, especially in relation to novel ecosystems and environmental change in Asia.
  </p>

  <p>
    Through this perspective, I seek to connect ecological processes with the cultural, institutional, and economic contexts in which environmental management takes place.
  </p>
